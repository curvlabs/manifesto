CURV — BRAND DIRECTION

Your identity is not your health.

It's everything else you do with it.

Identity

Curv makes healthcare products for people whose identity isn't their health, it's everything else they do with it.

Curv's products free you to build that identity.

The products are either so beautiful they're worth wearing anyway, or they're invisible.

In both cases, the health just happens.

The Enemy

Endless optimisation is joyless, and sexless.

Whoop, Oura etc. enslave you to a score, dehumanised/desexualised in the name of optimization. What's the point of being healthier, if it doesn't make you more graceful, human.

To add insult to injury, you must wear their ugly jewellery openly.

It marks you as a bro who's going to pull out his phone on a hinge date and stick it in the poor girls face, showing her all his stats. Tech is not cool. Tech is a tool. Don't make it the identity.

The Body

At its healthiest, the body is an instrument to play.

Curv's purpose is to restore your agency to use your body/play your instrument to whatever tune you like, by dealing with your health issues, with wearable technology.

We are here to increase your agency, not decrease it by anchoring you to some score.

You don't need to optimise, you just need a nudge here and there, when it matters.

Leading an interesting life. The health just happens.

The Persona

People who lead interesting lives are timelessly attractive. Good health restores your agency (ability to act independently), freeing you to lead an interesting life. Health is the crown only the sick can see, independence is the jewel in that crown. Obsession with one's health to the point of dependence on a score (i.e. a decrease in agency) is unattractive. They may still run, bike etc., but it is either as a means of health (production), or for a greater purpose.

A put together person, like a duck crossing a pond, appears to glide through life on the surface, but their legs are working underneath, invisible to the observer. The work is happening but it's nobody's business. Likewise, the health just happens. They're out to have fun, not talk about work.

Health as Ornamentation

Curv makes devices which are either so beautiful you'd want to wear them anyway, or are completely invisible.

Something worth wearing that happens to know your body. The product belongs in the same category as backless dress chains, silver nails, jewellery — objects of desire. It just so happens that they secretly contain your health.

The price is part of the aesthetic. Almost prohibitively expensive. Indifferent to men.

Insinuation of Sex

The bare spine is a seductive, elegant part of the body.

The shots are suggestive, but never sexual. Never degrade to selling sex. The only thing more powerful than sex is the insinuation of its possibility. Use that as hook/bait to get the audience engaged. Presence of a body. A pan stops before the reveal. Fabric, breath, the sound of a room with someone in it. The subject exists independently of you. You just happen to be seeing them.

References: Pavel Golik.

Unmanaged Authenticity of London/Paris/European Skate, Art Crowd

Kate Moss smoking isn't despite the cigarette being bad for her. It's because she doesn't need your approval. She's not cool because she's rebelling, she's indifferent. Rather than reframing back pain as sexy (it isn't), reframe the person as the kind of person who handles things quietly, without performing wellness to anyone. Anti-Lululemon, optimization-bro, 10,000 step-ification of youth culture. Of course, Kate doesn't wear a medical device, and would likely never be caught tracking anything publicly. But she certainly may have to privately, taking us back to the "it's none of your business" persona.

Rebelling Against the Corporate Overlords

The reason any of us have back pain is because we're chained to a desk for 40 hours a week doing our little laptop jobs, producing for our corporate overlords. It's truly not our fault. Poking fun at this in the right way could be volcanic. Possible merge with the unbothered London aesthetic via tongue in cheek humour.

Reference: Palace tongue in cheek humour.

The Pan

A slow pan down a bare spine. Expecting to see everything, but what you see instead is the tape, slowly entering the frame from the top, laying gently along the spine. Maybe you start with seeing hands on a person's back, and realise they're putting Curv on their partner.

The pan reaches the base of the back, stops just before revealing anything else. Then the dots animate on the spine.

The expectation is sexual, the reveal is clinical. Not sure if people will respond to that well, given they want sex, and it's been taken away from them. Rage bait perhaps.

The Unsolved Brief

The structural question that needs answering:

What does the viewer think they're watching in the first five seconds, and what do they realise they're actually watching by the end?

Every great product launch film has a turn. Before the turn they think they're watching one thing. After it they realise they're watching something else.

The Spine Strip

Flexible sensor tape that runs down the spine. Like the new backless dress chain — already fashion, just with a nervous system added. The form factor is inherently intimate. The spine is where someone touches you when they're being tender.

Embedding health in what you wear naturally, in a way that is unknown or unexpected. It's about the fashion. That's what matters.

The NFC Nail

Two NFC nails which say:

"Scan me if you love me" — gives them your number.

"Scan me if I'm dying" — gives them your emergency health info (diabetes etc.)

Desire and mortality sitting next to each other on two different fingers. That ambiguity is the whole point.

Products that encourage you to lead a better life. The purest expression of the manifesto: the health just happens.

The World
