CURV — BRAND DIRECTION

Your identity is not your health.

It's everything else you do with it.

The Belief

With Curv, the health just happens.

The products are either something worth wearing anyway, or completely out of sight, out of mind.

Our customers' identity isn't health, it's everything else they do with it.

Curv makes products to free you to build that identity.

Like a chic put together person, or a duck crossing a pond, composed on the surface, legs working underneath. The work is happening but it's nobody's business.

The health just happens.

The Enemy

The joylessness of optimisation. Endless optimisation is sexless.

Whoop, Oura etc. make you a slave to this score. Your body becomes dehumanised/desexualised in the name of optimization. What's the point of being healthier, if it doesn't make you more human, sexier, more effortless.

To add insult to injury, after asking all this of your identity, they give you this ugly jewellery.

It marks you as a bro who's going to pull out his phone on a hinge date and stick it in the poor girls face, showing her all his stats. Tech is not cool. Tech is a tool. Don't make it the identity.

The Belief

Your body is an instrument of play. At its best, it oozes sprezzatura. It is not a fragile glass house, to be handled delicately, at risk of being dropped and smashed into a million pieces.

Curv makes products which encourage you to use your body for whatever you want.

We are here to increase your agency, not decrease it by anchoring you to some score.

You don't need to optimise, you just need a nudge here and there, when it matters.

Leading an interesting life. The health just happens.

Insinuation of Sex

People who lead interesting lives are timelessly attractive. Good health restores your agency (ability to act independently), freeing you to lead an interesting life. Health is the crown only the sick can see, independence is the jewel in that crown. Obsession with one's health to the point of dependence on a score (i.e. a decrease in agency) is unattractive.

The shots are suggestive, but never sexual. The pan stops before the reveal. The status comes from what you withhold. The bare spine is an elegant, suggestive part of the body. Exactly what young healthy people look like.

Sound design is minimal: Fabric, breath, the sound of a room with someone in it. The subject exists independently of you. You just happen to be seeing them.

References: Wolfgang Tillmans, Isamaya Ffrench. Paloma Wool by Pavel Golik.

Unmanaged Authenticity of London Skater, Fashion Crowd

Kate Moss smoking isn't despite the cigarette being bad for her, it's because she doesn't need your approval about it. She's cool because she's indifferent.

Rather than reframing back pain as sexy (it isn't), reframe the person as the kind of person who handles things quietly, without performing wellness at anyone. Anti-Lululemon, optimization-bro, "I just did 10,000 steps"-ification of youth culture.

Of course, Kate Moss doesn't wear a medical device, and would never be caught tracking anything. That's a design problem though.

Health as Ornamentation

Curv makes devices which are either so beautiful you'd want to wear them anyway, or are completely invisible.

Something worth wearing that happens to know your body. The product belongs in the same category as backless dress chains, silver nails, jewellery — objects of desire. It just so happens that they secretly contain your health.

The price is part of the aesthetic. Almost prohibitively expensive. Indifferent to men.

The Pan

A slow pan down a bare spine, starting from the top. But what you see instead is the tape, slowly entering the frame from the top, laying gently along the spine.

The pan reaches the base of the back, stops just before revealing anything else. Then the dots come on — like a pulse landing.

Just skin, the strip, and the dots activating. The only thing more powerful than sex is the insinuation of its possibility. The reveal is clinical where the expectation is sexual.

The Bedroom

Two people. She's in front of him, takes her top off. She's shot from behind. He feels the strip on her back. He already knows, or doesn't care to ask. He just makes a quip about making her back pain worse. She giggles. That's it.

The sensor is just part of her the way a tattoo would be.

Versions with two guys, two girls, and a straight couple where the guy is wearing it — all played the same way. Nobody comments on the queerness or the gender flip. It's just Tuesday.

The Club

She's in a club. Meets someone. Gives him her number via the nail — "scan me if you love me." Later that night she nearly passes out. Someone panics, scans the other nail — "scan me if I'm dying." Emergency info comes up, help is called.

He's already in her life because he scanned the first nail hours earlier. He shows up at the hospital with flowers.

Same object. Same night. Both functions activated. The nail saved her life. It also got her the guy.

And she was just living.

The Unsolved Brief

The structural question that needs answering:

What does the viewer think they're watching in the first five seconds, and what do they realise they're actually watching by the end?

Every great product launch film has a turn. Before the turn they think they're watching one thing. After it they realise they're watching something else.

The Spine Strip

Flexible sensor tape that runs down the spine. Like the new backless dress chain — already fashion, just with a nervous system added. The form factor is inherently intimate. The spine is where someone touches you when they're being tender.

Embedding health in what you wear naturally, in a way that is unknown or unexpected. It's about the fashion. That's what matters.

The NFC Nail

A silver nail you wear. Two versions that look identical from the outside:

"Scan me if you love me" — gives your number.

"Scan me if I'm dying" — gives your health info.

Desire and mortality sitting next to each other on the same finger, indistinguishable from the outside. Nobody knows which nail is which. That ambiguity is the whole point.

Jewellery that contains you. The purest expression of the manifesto: the health just happens.
